Grammar Note: Polish Numbers & Cases

üîπ Why 1 ‚Üí nominative singular

In Polish (and other Slavic languages), the number 1 behaves like an adjective:
jeden kot = "one cat" ‚Üí "kot" stays in nominative singular, just like it would without a number.
It agrees directly with the noun, like ten kot ("this cat").

üîπ Why 2, 3, 4 ‚Üí nominative plural

Originally in Old Slavic, small numbers like 2, 3, 4 had a dual form (between singular & plural).
Polish lost the dual, but the pattern stayed: Numbers 2, 3, 4 behave as "close to singular", so the noun looks like nominative plural.
Example: trzy psy ("three dogs") ‚Üí normal plural.

üî∫ Why 5 and above ‚Üí genitive plural

Numbers ‚â•5 were treated as a collective quantifier (‚Äúa group of X‚Äù), not a direct count.
So instead of agreeing like an adjective, they govern the genitive case, like ‚Äúmany‚Äù:
wiele ps√≥w = "many dogs"
piƒôƒá ps√≥w = "five dogs"
Literally: ‚Äúa group of 5 (of) cats‚Äù ‚Üí genitive plural.

üî∫ Why 0 ‚Üí genitive plural

Zero is treated like "none of", which also uses the genitive.
Example: zero problem√≥w = "zero problems".

üî∫ Why 12, 13, 14 are exceptions

Although ending in 2, 3, 4, these are teen numbers (from dwa-na≈õcie, trzy-na≈õcie).
‚ÄúNa≈õcie‚Äù numbers behave like ‚â•5 ‚Üí force genitive plural:
12 ps√≥w, 13 ps√≥w, 14 ps√≥w.

üìå Summary

  • 1 ‚Üí nominative singular
  • 2, 3, 4 ‚Üí nominative plural
  • 5+ and 0 ‚Üí genitive plural
  • 12‚Äì14 ‚Üí genitive plural (exception)
